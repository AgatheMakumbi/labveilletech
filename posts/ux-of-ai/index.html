The UX of AI: Patterns and Mental Models

If you’re reading this, you’re probably tech-curious and familiar with AI chatbots. Actually, these days everyone is using tools like ChatGPT, Gemini, or Copilot.

We might love one and get frustrated with another, even when the interfaces look similar. This is where UX of AI becomes fascinating a fresh field that’s redefining how we design intelligent systems.

These Patterns Shape Our Experience

When I started researching, I discovered tiny interaction patterns that make or break our AI experience: inputs, suggestions, “stream of thought” responses, attachments, filters, voice & tone…

Emily Campbell’s “The Shape of AI” is a brilliant resource, it documents 50+ patterns across AI tools, with detailed breakdowns, best practices, and real product examples. It’s especially valuable for teams building their own AI-powered products.

UX Starts When Designing the Model

A truly user-centric AI product starts at the modelling stage. Greg Nudelman (a leading voice in AI UX) explains this perfectly with his Digital Twin concept:

“Digital Twin is simply a digital model of the real world that focuses on modelling the metrics and outcomes that matter most to a physical system. It’s an excellent model for UX Design of AI-driven products.” (Nudelman, 2025)

The Digital Twin is a powerful UX tool.

It’s a mental map of the AI product that helps you understand:

  • What the system sees/receives
  • What it understands
  • What it decides and what the user receives.

This makes you think: “What real-world problem is my AI supposed to improve?" “What information does it need to do that?" “What output does the user get?”

These reflections help communicate to users how the AI can help and what its limits are. They also let you define and anticipate moments of truth (when the AI or interface messages need to step in).

Without understanding what your AI perceives, you can’t explain:

  • Why it makes certain recommendations
  • Why it gets things wrong
  • What it completely ignores

A Rapidly Evolving Field

The literature on this topic is growing steadily (even if it’s still quite new). Some say we’re entering the era of true intelligence; others think AI is just a passing trend.

Either way, usages change fast, and as UX designers, we must keep asking these fundamental questions to build products users actually need.
